"Nothing special," she lied and hated herself a little for it. It was a small lie, harmless. And yet she felt a wall building between them, stone by stone, day by day. Words that remained unspoken, thoughts she kept to herself -- not out of malice or calculation, but out of a strange shyness, a fear of disappointment. His or her own, she wasn't sure.

Julian accepted her answer with a nod, no follow-up question, no insistence. Was that respect for her boundaries or indifference? She wondered if he sensed that she was lying, if it hurt him. Or if he simply didn't want to know what was really going on inside her.

They drank their coffee silently, each lost in thought. The clock on the wall ticked, marking the seconds that passed unused between them. "I have to go," Julian finally said and put his empty cup in the sink. He had a workshop to lead early today, Lena remembered. Something about creative writing for high school students.

"Of course." She stepped aside to let him pass, although the kitchen was large enough that there was no need for it. "Will we see each other tonight?" He paused briefly, his hand on her shoulder, like an anchor. "Sophie asked if we want to go to the movies."

Lena had forgotten, or repressed it. She liked Sophie, really. Her friend's lively, direct manner was a welcome counterbalance to her own introversion. But lately she found it hard to muster the energy for social interactions, even with people she liked.

"Sounds good," she said nevertheless, another small lie, another stone in the wall. Julian kissed her fleetingly on the forehead, a gesture that seemed both tender and distant. "I'm looking forward to it. See you later then."

She heard him go to the bathroom, turn on the shower. The familiar rhythm of the morning continued as if nothing was different than usual. She finished her coffee, took a notepad from the kitchen table -- Julian had the habit of distributing notepads everywhere in the apartment, always ready to capture a thought -- and absentmindedly scribbled on the paper. Not really words, more like shapes, spirals, lines that merged into each other. Her hand moved as if it had a will of its own.

How much easier it would be if she could simply write down what was going on inside her. But for that she would first have to understand it herself. Later, after Julian had left and she had gotten herself ready for the day, she sorted her documents for the seminars. She had a packed day today: Literary Theory in the morning, a workshop on German Romanticism in the afternoon, in between research for her master's thesis.

Actually, she should have been looking forward to the Romanticism session -- Novalis, Eichendorff, the longing for the infinite that pulsed in their texts. Themes that had once filled her with enthusiasm. But now she felt only a strange indifference. As if something had slipped between her and literature, a thin but impenetrable layer of... of what? She pushed the question aside, packed her bag and left Julian's apartment.

The cold November air greeted her like a shock, waking her from her numbness. She breathed deeply, let the cold fill her lungs, felt how her body reacted to it, contracted, protected itself. Maybe that was what she needed -- a wake-up call, something that broke through the numbness that had slowly settled over her life.

The campus was lively, as always at this time of day. Students streamed to their lectures, some hurried with coffee cups in hand, others strolled in groups, absorbed in conversations. Lena mingled among them, anonymous, invisible.

In the library she found a place in a quiet corner, spread out her documents. She wanted to use the hour until her first seminar to go through the last notes for her presentation on intertextual references in Virginia Woolf. It was a topic that had connected her and Julian, back when their relationship still consisted of secret notes in book margins, of shared thoughts that seemed more fleeting and yet somehow more substantial than their conversations today.

She sometimes missed that -- the tension of the unknown, the discovery of a new world of thought. Now they knew each other, or should at least. And yet she had the feeling that something essential had been lost between them, just as they had found each other.
