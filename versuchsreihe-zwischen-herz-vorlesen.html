Chapter 1: The Message That Changed Everything

The library was, as always, a refuge. Lena Winter breathed in deeply the familiar smell of old paper as her gaze wandered through the room. The silence of a Thursday afternoon was a pleasant contrast to the loud bustle outside, where other students stood together in groups, laughing and talking - a social game in which she never had the right cards in her hand.

"Why is it so hard for me to talk to others?" she asked herself for the hundredth time. This question constantly gnawed at her, especially when she observed other students who had already formed close friendships after just three months at university. Friendships that looked like something real, not like the superficial acquaintances she had had in school.

She ran her hand over the spines in the German literature section, her heartbeat gradually calming. Professor Kleinschmidt had given them a list of primary literature for the seminar "Realism and Social Currents." Unlike her fellow students, who read everything digitally, Lena needed the feeling of paper under her fingers - a connection to the past that gave her support when the present became too complicated.

That was one of the reasons why she had decided on literary studies - here she could communicate with people who had long since died, without stuttering or turning red. Through literature, she understood the world better than through direct experience. "You're wasting your best years between dusty books," her mother had said during her last visit. The tone worried, the eyes full of incomprehension. "Life happens out there, not on paper pages."

Lena bit her lip. It was her third semester, and while her roommate raved about last weekend - some guy named Marc and too much vodka - and her former school friend Sophie posted photos of dates every other day, she spent her Friday evenings with fictional characters. "I'm twenty-one and have never had a real boyfriend," she whispered so quietly that no one could hear.
