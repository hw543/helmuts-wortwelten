What would Sophie say? Probably: "You just have to open up, Lena. How is anyone supposed to get to know you if you hide behind your books?" But why did every "Hey, do you want to have coffee with us?" feel like an exam she hadn't studied for? Why could she only really breathe in these quiet moments between bookshelves? Nevertheless, she sometimes couldn't ignore the quiet voice that asked whether she wasn't missing something. Whether there was someone out there who would understand her if she only had the courage to open up.

"Excuse me," someone whispered beside her, and Lena jumped as if she had been torn from a dream. A young man with short dark hair and rimless glasses stood there. His eyes - dark brown with an interesting golden shimmer - met hers, and for a moment she forgot to breathe. He smiled, a crooked, apologetic smile that touched something in her. "I need to get to the shelf." His voice was deep and pleasant, with a warmth she hadn't expected.

"Oh, of course." Lena hastily stepped aside and bumped into a bookshelf in the process. Several volumes threatened to fall, and she grabbed for them frantically while her face turned the color of an overripe tomato. 'Fantastic, Lena. That's how you make an impression.' The student skillfully caught one of the falling volumes. "Seems like Fontane really wants attention," he said with a wink that made her heartbeat accelerate.

He pulled out a book and looked at it briefly. Then he looked at her again, seemed to hesitate for a moment as if he wanted to say something, but then just nodded at her friendly and left. Lena didn't really breathe again until he was out of sight. These brief social interactions were a minefield, and she had just once again proven why she preferred communicating with books rather than people. Twenty-one years old and still unable to speak to an attractive man without making herself a complete idiot.

'You should get out among people more often,' Sophie had said before she moved to another city. 'How else will you ever meet someone?' Lena sighed and turned her attention back to the books. Her hand stopped at "Effi Briest" - Fontane's masterpiece about a young woman who breaks under the suffocating conventions of her time. She pulled out the book. 'Maybe you're like Effi,' whispered a voice in her head. 'Trapped between what you want and what you think you should be.'

The book was an older edition, the cover worn, the pages yellowed, as if it had already shared hundreds of stories with other readers. How many lonely students had probably already lost themselves in Effi's story? How many had recognized themselves in her? With the book in her hand, she went to a secluded reading spot by the window. The afternoon sun bathed everything in warm light, and for a moment she wondered if the student with the glasses had been looking for the book she now held in her hands.

Lena sat down, opened the book and immersed herself in Effi's story - the story of a young woman who marries without knowing what love actually means, who puts social expectations above her own needs and breaks because of it. 'What would I do?' she asked herself. 'If I had to choose between heart and mind?' When she got to chapter five, her gaze caught on something unusual. In the margin of page 27 was a handwritten note in blue ink, the handwriting neat and almost elegant: "Isn't it strange how Effi is torn between social expectations and her own desires? Some things never change, do they? -- J."

Lena stared at the note, her heartbeat accelerated. Writing in library books was an unwritten taboo - but this remark was so apt, so personal, as if someone had written down her own thoughts. J. Who was J.? Male? Female? Someone who asked the same questions about life as she did? It wasn't an ordinary scribble or trivial comment like you sometimes found in textbooks. These words came from someone who didn't just read, but felt, who read between the lines and drew parallels to their own world.

This mysterious "J." posed a question that seemed directed at future readers - at her. She read the text passage next to which the note stood. Effi and Innstetten see each other again after his proposal - two people who will marry without knowing each other, without knowing if their hearts beat in the same rhythm. 'How many people still do that today?' thought Lena. 'Being together because it's expected, not because it feels right?'

She thought of her last dorm party when Sophie tried to persuade her to flirt with that business student. "He's perfect for you," she had said. "His parents have a law firm, he drives an Audi..." As if those were the things that mattered. As if love were a checklist. A sudden impulse came over her. This unknown J. had touched something in her, struck a chord that resonated. Before she could lose her courage, she took out her ballpoint pen and wrote directly under the note: "The forms change, but the pressure remains. Today we tear ourselves apart between authenticity and self-optimization. Progress? -- L."

Her hand trembled slightly as she put the pen down. She, Lena Winter, the epitome of rule conformity, had just defaced a library book! A wave of guilt came over her, followed by an unexpected tingle of excitement. It felt like a secret, an intimate moment between two strangers who felt connected by nothing but their thoughts. This unknown J. had written something that spoke directly to her soul, and she had answered - not after hours of deliberation, not after weighing every word, but spontaneously and honestly.

'Is that the difference?' she asked herself. 'Between a conversation with a stranger on a bus and words on paper? The absence of gazes, the freedom from expectations?' She tried to continue reading, but her eyes kept wandering back to the little exchange of letters in the margin. Had she just opened an invisible door? And if so - where would it lead? The thought that this unknown J. might one day read her answer triggered an unfamiliar tingling in her stomach. Like a modern message in a bottle, thrown into a sea of books.

Who was J.? Her mind painted possible faces: Was it perhaps that handsome literature student from the fourth semester who always sat in the front row and asked clever questions? The one with the intense eyes and quiet laughter who sometimes sat alone in the cafeteria reading? Or was J. a woman, perhaps that confident student with short blue hair who had given a passionate speech about the suppression of female voices in literature in the last seminar?

The handwriting revealed little - neat, flowing, with a certain elegance, but not affected. Not the shaky writing of a professor on the verge of retirement, but also not the hasty scribble of a disinterested freshman. And why had J. wanted to capture precisely this thought? Was this person also sometimes lonely among people? Did they also know this feeling of not really belonging, of being different? The possibilities were endless, and this uncertainty had something strangely exciting about it.

After a while she closed the book and looked at her watch. She was shocked to find that she had been reading for over two hours. The library had emptied, only scattered students still sat at the tables. Lena wondered if she should borrow "Effi Briest." The story had captivated her, and she wanted to know how it continued. But then J. might never read her answer if the book lay at her home for weeks.

The thought hit her with unexpected intensity: She wanted J. to find her answer. She wanted to continue this dialogue, this strange, anonymous connection that felt more real than many of her daily interactions. 'What's wrong with you, Lena?' she asked herself. 'You're fantasizing about someone you've never met, based on a single sentence in a book?' And yet she put the book back on the shelf, carefully making sure it was easy to find, the spine protruding slightly between the others. As if she were leaving a secret sign. 'I'll come back tomorrow,' she decided, surprised by her own determination.

Band 2: Revealed Truths
Chapter 1: Proximity that Flees

Lena awoke with the feeling that something was missing. The warmth beside her was palpable, physically present, breathing, real. Julian's arm lay heavily across her waist, his breath steady and deep against her neck. And yet there existed a strange emptiness that she couldn't grasp. She blinked into the early morning light that fell through the not quite closed curtains and tried to locate the sensation. It was like a word on the tip of her tongue -- present, but unreachable.

Three months had passed since the first kiss by the river. Three months in which hesitant touches had become familiarity, shared books had become a shared bed, two separate lives had become a common everyday life. Everything had developed so naturally, so effortlessly, as if it were a story that had only been waiting to be told. Julian murmured something in his sleep, pulled her closer to him. She felt his heartbeat through the thin fabric of her T-shirt, constant and reassuring.

She should be happy. She was happy. Why then this nagging feeling of incompleteness? Carefully she freed herself from his embrace, careful not to wake him. On tiptoes she crept out of the bedroom, crossed the narrow hallway of his apartment and entered the kitchen. His kitchen. Not hers. She now spent more time here than in her own apartment, but she hadn't given hers up. Not yet. A small, peculiar resistance that she didn't quite understand herself.

She made coffee and watched how the water dripped drop by drop and patiently through the coffee grounds. There was comfort in these morning rituals, something reliable. The smell of fresh coffee filled the room, displacing for a moment the indefinite restlessness in her. She opened the kitchen window. Outside, the city still lay in half-sleep, although the first cars were already driving through the streets, early commuters who wanted to be ahead of the rush hour traffic.

November had swept the trees almost bare, only individual stubborn leaves still clung to the branches. A bit like her to her own apartment, Lena thought and smiled at the strange comparison. "Good morning." Julian's voice made her jump. He stood in the doorway, in boxer shorts and T-shirt, his hair tousled from sleep, his face still soft from dreams. "Sorry, I didn't want to scare you." He came to her, wrapped his arms around her waist from behind and pressed a kiss on her shoulder. "You were gone."

"I wanted to make coffee," she explained and pointed to the coffee machine, which was just releasing its last drops into the pot. "Hmm." He buried his face in her hair and breathed deeply. "You smell good." "I smell like sleep," she corrected with a slight laugh. "Exactly." His hands wandered under her T-shirt, gently caressed her stomach. His touch was warm, familiar, sent small waves of arousal through her body. She instinctively leaned against him, let her head sink onto his shoulder.

This was simple. This worked. The physical part of their relationship had never caused the problems she had initially feared. No insecurity, no shame, no disappointment. Their bodies had found a language from the beginning that needed no translation. Julian turned her to him, kissed the corner of her mouth, her cheek, finally her mouth. She returned the kiss, let her fingers glide through his hair, enjoyed the warmth that built up between them.

When they separated from each other, he smiled. "Good morning," he repeated, this time with a deeper meaning in his voice. "Good morning," she answered and tried to feel the echo of his smile on her own face. "Coffee?" "Please." He separated from her, reached for two cups from the cupboard. Routine movements, practiced dance. He knew how she liked her coffee -- black, no sugar -- how she needed the silence in the morning before she could speak, how her body wanted to be touched.

He knew her. And yet she sometimes had the feeling that something essential was missing, that they looked past each other even though they looked directly into each other's eyes. "What are you thinking about?" Julian's question interrupted her thoughts. He handed her a cup, the warmth penetrated through the porcelain into her hands. "Nothing special," she lied and hated herself a little for it. It was a small lie, harmless. And yet she felt how a wall was building up between them, stone by stone, day by day.

Words that remained unspoken, thoughts that she kept to herself -- not out of malice or calculation, but out of a strange shyness, a fear of disappointment. His or her own, she wasn't sure. Julian accepted her answer with a nod, no follow-up question, no insistence. Was that respect for her boundaries or indifference? She wondered if he sensed that she was lying, if it hurt him. Or if he simply didn't want to know what was really going on inside her.

They drank their coffee in silence, each lost in thought. The clock on the wall ticked, marking the seconds that passed unused between them. "I have to go," Julian finally said and put his empty cup in the sink. He had a workshop to lead early today, Lena remembered. Something about creative writing for high school students. "Of course." She stepped aside to let him pass, although the kitchen was large enough that there was no need for it.

"Will we see each other tonight?" He stopped briefly, his hand on her shoulder, like an anchor. "Sophie asked if we wanted to go to the movies." Lena had forgotten, or suppressed it. She liked Sophie, really. The lively, direct manner of her friend was a welcome counterweight to her own introversion. But lately it was difficult for her to muster the energy for social interactions, even with people she liked. "Sounds good," she said nevertheless, again a small lie, again a stone in the wall.

Julian kissed her fleetingly on the forehead, a gesture that seemed both tender and distant at the same time. "I'm looking forward to it. See you later then." She heard him go into the bathroom, turn on the shower. The familiar rhythm of the morning continued as if nothing was different than usual. She finished her coffee, took a notepad from the kitchen table -- Julian had the habit of distributing notepads everywhere in the apartment, always ready to capture a thought -- and scribbled absently on the paper.

Not really words, rather shapes, spirals, lines that merged into each other. Her hand moved as if it had a will of its own. How much easier it would be if she could simply write down what was going on inside her. But for that she would first have to understand it herself. Later, after Julian had left and she had made herself ready for the day, she sorted her documents for the seminars. She had a packed day today: literary theory in the morning, a workshop on German Romanticism in the afternoon, research for her master's thesis in between.

Actually, she should have been looking forward to the Romanticism session -- Novalis, Eichendorff, the longing for the infinite that pulsed in their texts. Topics that had once filled her with enthusiasm. But now she felt only a strange indifference. As if something had slipped between her and literature, a thin but impenetrable layer of... of what? She pushed the question aside, packed her bag and left Julian's apartment.

The cold November air greeted her like a shock, woke her from her numbness. She breathed deeply, let the cold fill her lungs, felt how her body reacted to it, contracted, protected itself. Perhaps that was what she needed -- a wake-up call, something that broke through the numbness that had slowly settled over her life.
